DESIGN AND DEVELOPMENT OF A YOLO-BASED SPEED DETECTION AND MONITORING
SYSTEM FOR LOW-LIGHT TRAFFIC MONITORING

Abstract

This study addresses the lack of reliable nighttime vehicle speed
monitoring in the Philippines through the design and development of NOVA
(Nighttime Observation and Vehicle Analysis), an AI-based speed
detection and monitoring system for low-light traffic conditions. The
system was implemented at 283 MH Del Pilar St., 5th Ave., Brgy. 117,
Caloocan City.

The study is significant to barangay-level traffic enforcement units and
the Metropolitan Manila Development Authority (MMDA). A quantitative
experimental research design was employed, consisting of eight
controlled experiments varying camera mounting height, resolution,
brightness level, training dataset size, training epochs, confidence
threshold, and speed sensitivity. Statistical analyses included One-Way
ANOVA, Independent Samples T-Test, and Repeated Measures ANOVA. A
5-point Likert scale survey involving ten MMDA officers was conducted to
evaluate system usability and effectiveness.

Results showed that a 4.0-meter mounting height yielded optimal
detection performance with 98.96% Precision, 97.34% Recall, and 97.83%
F1 Score. No significant difference was found between 720p and 1080p
resolutions, indicating that 720p is a cost-effective alternative. A 50%
brightness level produced optimal detection, while severe overexposure
at 90–100% resulted in detection failure.

Training with 5,000 images significantly outperformed training with
1,000 images, achieving mAP@50-95 of 0.839. Extending training to 150
epochs produced significant localization improvements only for the
larger dataset. YOLOv11-Large and YOLOv26-Large achieved optimal
detection performance within the 60–80% confidence threshold range. A
sensitivity setting of 17.0 produced the lowest stable mean speed error
of 8%. The system received an overall acceptability rating of 4.73 out
of 5.00.

The researchers recommend further evaluation of brightness thresholds
using measured illuminance levels in lux under actual nighttime field
conditions to determine system detection limits more accurately.

Keywords: nighttime traffic monitoring, vehicle speed detection, YOLO
object detection, low-light surveillance, CCTV-based monitoring, deep
learning, computer vision, traffic enforcement

Description of Research Instrument Used

This section describes the experimental procedures conducted to evaluate
the NOVA speed detection system under low-light conditions. Eight
experiments assessed the effects of camera mounting height, resolution,
illumination level, dataset size, training epochs, confidence threshold,
and AI model configuration using YOLOv8, YOLOv11, and YOLOv26.

Experiment 1: Effect of Camera Mounting Height on Detection Clarity The
independent variable was camera height (3m, 3.5m, 4m, 4.5m, 5m). The
dependent variable was vehicle bounding box size (pixels) and detection
clarity. One-Way ANOVA determined statistical differences among mounting
heights.

Experiment 2: Effect of Camera Resolution on Detection Accuracy
Resolutions tested were 720p and 1080p. The dependent variables were
accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score. Performance comparisons
determined the optimal balance between computational efficiency and
detection performance.

Experiment 3: Effect of Image Brightness Level on Detection Accuracy
Brightness levels from 0% to 100% were tested under controlled nighttime
conditions. Detection metrics included precision, recall, and F1-score.
Statistical tests determined whether illumination significantly affected
performance and identified minimum acceptable lighting conditions.

Experiment 4: Effect of Dataset Size on Model Validation Accuracy Two
datasets containing 1,000 and 5,000 images were used. The dependent
variable was validation accuracy. Independent Samples T-Test determined
statistical significance between dataset sizes.

Experiment 5: Effect of Training Epochs on Model Accuracy and Loss
Training durations were varied up to 150 epochs. Validation loss and
accuracy were analyzed using Repeated Measures ANOVA to determine
performance improvements and convergence stability.

Experiment 6: Effect of Confidence Threshold on Detection Precision and
Recall Confidence thresholds ranging from 60% to 90% were evaluated.
One-Way ANOVA identified the optimal threshold balancing false positives
and false negatives.

Experiment 7: Comparison of AI Detection and Manual Ground Truth Speed
sensitivity settings were evaluated using Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and
Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE). Statistical comparison verified
agreement between AI-detected speeds and manually measured ground truth
values.

Evaluation Instrument on the Effectiveness and Usability of the NOVA
System

Ten respondents evaluated the NOVA system using a structured 5-point
Likert scale questionnaire covering:

1.  System Usefulness
2.  System Usability
3.  User Satisfaction
4.  Intention to Continue Use

Weighted mean scores were computed and interpreted against a rating
scale. The grand mean score of 4.73 indicates that the NOVA system is
effective, reliable, and suitable for nighttime vehicle speed detection
under low-light traffic conditions.
